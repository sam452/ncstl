by Joe Bozeman

 

I have been a visitor of Allatoona Pass for many years. When I was in high school, it was a wonderful place to park, drink adult beverages, and try to make contact with the ghost of the Unknown Hero. My Grandfather and Uncle, both NC&StL conductors, always told me,  Son, you had better stay away from that place at night, theres a haint in that cut.

 

Since joining the NCPS, The Pass has taken on a new meaning to me. I go there often and am always amazed at the feeling I get.  I stand in the cut and am awed at the physical labor it took to move the dirt and rock to make way for the roadbed.  I visualize Andrews and his band of spies racing northbound in their attempt to destroy the W&A. I think of Captain Fuller rolling through on his handcar in hot pursuit.  I listen to the howling wind and think of what a sight it must have been to see a Stripe hugging the walls with a full head of steam; its whistle echoing through the cut. I think of the Confederate and Union soldiers attacking and defending this vital rail link during the War for Southern Independence.  I still look for the ghost of the Unknown Hero. I have felt his presence, but  have never seen him.Allatoona Pass was a travel hub before the railroad was

neering marvel of its time.  Only the bore at Tunnel Hill was more difficult to build.

 

The Pass fell into Union hands in 1864, and was the perfect spot for the Confederates to cut General Shermans supply line to Atlanta. On October 5, 1864, Confederate troops attacked the Union forces at Allatoona. Their mission was to destroy The Pass and burn the Etowah River Bridge; this would have isolated Sherman and his army in Atlanta.  The result was a disaster for both sides, but the Union held The Pass.  Of 5301 men engaged in the battle, there were 1603 casualties.  The Union troop loss was 35% and the Confederates was 27% for a combined percentage of casualties equaled only by the Battle of Gettysburg.
