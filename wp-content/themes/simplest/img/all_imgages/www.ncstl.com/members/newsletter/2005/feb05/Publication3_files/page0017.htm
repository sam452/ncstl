NC&StL Ry News Item

October 1926

 

By W. H. Smith

 

In looking over the last issue of our Time-table and Schedules I was struck with the size and volume of present day issues when compared to what they were when I first entered the service.

 

In those days they were printed on a single sheet of paper about ten by twelve inches showing the schedules on one side and the rules on the other.  I remember one old rule reading as follows: The pusher may ply between Cowan and Tantallon regardless of all trains.  This, in effect, meant the trains must hunt the pusher, instead of the pusher hunting the trains.  But our trains were then few in number and not very heavy, usually from seven to ten cars and probably not over three or four hundred tons in weight.  Yet we maintained a pusher at Cowan on a twenty-four hour basis in charge of Rufe Sartain who was a very close friend of Major Thomas, and he was known as the Mountain Boss, having charge of the pusher, the section men, the bridge men and most everything else between Cowan and Tantallon; and Tobe Stewart stood next to him as a sort of Train Dispatcher on that nine miles of track; and it was the duty of all train crews to wire Mr. Stewart at Cowan whether or not they would need the pusher, then Mr. Stewart would confer with Mr. Sartain and arrange the details.  If a southbound train needed the pusher Mr. Sartain would have steam up and shove them up to the tunnel.  In case of a northbound train arriving at Tantallon and not requiring the pusher, then Mr. Stewart and Mr. Sartain would wire the Conductor an order like this:  Come ahead to Cowan, we are holding the pusher until you get here, but lookout for them goats as you come out of the tunnel.

 

Them goats were the mountain trains using our tracks between the tunnel and Cowan.

 

In those days there was no such station as Sherwood, but that location, a simple road crossing, was shown in our Time-table as Crows Nest.  And about one and a half miles north of Crows Nest we maintained a lone telegraph office and a long siding known as Tantallon.

Another conspicuous rule on our old time card read as follows:  Any employee becoming habitually addicted to the use of intoxicating liquors will be dismissed  (an occasional drink didnt count).  But we had an old Baggage Master, whose name I wont call, but remembered by many old men now in the service, who concluded he would test the constitutionality of this rule.  So he got drunk coming in on Number Two and mixed up the baggage all the way from Wartrace to Nashville and when the train got here he was so limber that he had to be hauled out on the trucks with the rest of the baggage.  Needless to say he was discharged on the spot.  But he immediately left for Texas which was then the Mecca of discharged employees from all over the United State, and he quickly secured a job as brakesman between Galveston and San Antonio.  And when he drew his first months pay he had the brazen effrontery to sit down and wire Mr. Wrenne the following impudent telegram:  You discharged me for a very trivial offense but thank God I am now in Texas and have a better job; but if you still smell my breath just say so, and I will then pull up and move over into Mexico.

 

I mention these bygone experiences to show how different are the present methods of railroading when compared to the crude ways of fifty years ago, and it all goes to show that Grandpas Railroad is keeping abreast of the times and is growing better and better every day in every way.
