Wreck at Tullahoma, January 27, 1905

Nashville Banner, Friday evening, January 27, 1905

Passenger And Freight Train Collide.

FOUR MEN REPORTED KILLED.

Several Passengers Receive Injuries Of a More or Less Serious Nature.

Engines are Demolished

Special to the Banner, Tullahoma, Tenn., January 27.

This morning at 12:15 o’clock, No. 14, a northbound freight, collided with No. 3, a southbound passenger train, and swept four men into eternity, without warning. The collision wrecked two of the finest engines on the Nashville, Chattanooga & St. Louis Railway, and made kindling wood of the five freight cars, scattering merchandise in every direction. The men killed were:

ENGINEER JOSEPH SUGGS of the freight train.
ENGINEER WILLIAM RAY of the passenger train.
HORACE SUGGS, fireman on the passenger train.
JOHN RUSSELL, colored porter.

Father and Son.
The dead men all live in Nashville. One of the most pitiful incidents of the terrible wreck was that the two Suggs’ were father and son, the son being fireman on the No. 3 going south, while the father was driver on the freight, going north. The engines were little more than so much scrap iron. Four box cars were literally torn into splinters, and the merchandise was thrown up in a pile on top of the wreck.

Miraculous Escape.
The express car, with the through freight, on the passenger, was next to the engine and was telescoped to the tender. Just how Wm. O. King,the messenger, escaped without a scratch seems little less than a miracle.
Mr. King states that when the crashcame, he was seated in the middle of the car checking up his books, and before he knew what was taking place, he was hurled against the far end of the car and two crates containing two head of fine cattle consigned to Fitzgerald, Ga., were hanging immediately above. He crawled out without being hurt, and the cattle also escaped without a bruise.

How It Happened.
There are conflicting statements as to how fast the trains were traveling, but from the best sources it is safe to say that when the impact came the passenger was going at about fifteen miles an hour, and the freight was going a a good haps twenty miles an hour. The freight engineer was trying to make the siding, which is about 1,500 feet from the depot, ahead of the passenger,which was due at Tullahoma at 12:20 o’clock. The track where the catastrophe occured is perfectly straight for five or six miles, and it is incomprehensible as to how such an accident could take place, under the conditions.

Horribly Mangled.
The bodies of the dead men were horribly mangled and that of Engineer Sugg was almost burned to a crisp. The passengers on the train were very badly shaken up, but so far as known one were dangerously hurt. The following were among those who were painfully hurt:

  • J. H. Jordan, brakeman, who was on his way to Chattanooga to come out with his train, had a severe cut above the right eye and head bruised.
  • W. J. West, Sheriff of Scott County, home Huntsville, Tenn., had ankle cut, bruised and head hurt.
  • William Heddow, the conductor of the passenger, was badly shaken and bruised and it is thought he was internally injured.
  • W. C. Todd, Jingo, Williamson County, ankle hurt.
  • Morris J. Connelly, Atlanta, hurt on head, not serious.
  • L. T. Muse, Nashville, express messenger, hip hurt and back wrenched.
  • Sid Reynolds, baggagemaster, cut on wrist.
  • E. Clayton, colored porter on Pullman, head hurt.
  • Wynn Thomas of Nashville was slightly shaken up and bruised.
  • Capt. Blevins of Rogersville was hurt on the ankle and bruised, but not serious.

Narrow Escapes.
There were many who received minor wounds, but not enough to cause inconvenience. E. L. Grubbs, the fireman on

He states that Sugg, the engineer, called to him to jump, and then tried to jump himself, but was too late. W. McGee, a brakeman, was also on the freight engine, and saved himself by jumping. Hoodoo Engine.

The engines were passenger No. 254 and freight No. 183. They were both among the best engines on the Nashville, Chattanooga & St. Louis Railway, but 254 is regarded as a hoody, this being her third wreck. It was the most disastrous wreck ever had on the road. James Granberry was severely injured by flying bar from the wreck.

MISCALCULATION CAUSE

Engineer of Freight Evidently Did Not Know Passenger was So Close.

Reports at the headquarters of the Nashville, Chattanooga & St. Louis Railway in this city indicate that the wreck was caused by the engineer of the freight train pulling up and backing into the switch instead of heading in, as is the rule of the road. The freight was scheduled to meet the passenger train No. 3, which leaves this city at 9:50 p.m., at Tullahoma, as was taking the switch when the accident occurred. The siding is some distance this side of the station, but within the station limits.

The accident is one of those unexplained ones which often occur and will never be explained,but it is supposed that the engineer on the freight took it for granted that on account of the long stretch of straight track just this side of the station, he could be seen by the passenger engineer. But on account of the difficulty of telling on a straight track whether a train is moving or standing still he misjudged his distance and was struck before he could get his whole train into the siding. All the train but the engine and three cars had entered the switch when the passenger train, coming at a fast rate, struck head on, ditching the freight engine and three cars and derailing several cars of the passenger train.

The passenger suffered practically no smashing, only the break, besides the slight damage done the front of the engine, being the breaking out of one of the drawerheads on one of the baggage cars.

Trusted Employees.

The four employees who were killed were among the most trusted on the road. Joe B. Sugg, engineer of the freight train, was a veteran railroader and had been in the employ of the road for many years. He and his son, H. J. Sugg, fireman on the passenger, who was also killed, lived at 110 Fifteenth avenue, North. His wife and one son and two daughters survive him.

W. J. Ray, who was engineer of the passenger train,lived at 208 Fourteenth ave, North (Hardee street), and leaves a wife and several small children. John Burrows, who was the negro porter on the passenger train, was one of the most faithful servants in the service of the road. He was for some years the personal porter of Mr. M. J. C. Wrenne, Superintendent of Transportation of the Nashville, Chattanooga & St. Louis Railway, and in every capacity where he has served has shown himself to be respectful and competent.

L. M. Muse, express messenger, who was quite seriously hurt in the collision at Murfreesboro some weeks ago, was at first reported to be very seriously if not fatally hurt, but a later report says that Dr. Eve has examined him and finds his condition to be not so serious as at first thought. The old bruises and sprains which he received in the Murfreesboro wreck were hurt over again, and this, of course, causes him great pain. E. L. Grubbs, fireman of the freight, escaped injury.

Tracks Cleared.

The tracks were cleared at about 9:15 this morning, and traffic has been resumed as usual. The Chicago and Florida Limited passed through here about two hours late, and was delayed this side of Tullahoma until the track was cleared. The north-bound limited escaped the wreck and came in on time. Several of the railroad officials went from here to the scene of the accident in order to straighten out the trains and expedite the work of clearing away the debris. To the special relief train which tached the private car of General Manager John W. Thomas, Jr., who came in off the Northwestern Division early this morning. Supt. J. H. Peebles was also in the train which carried Dr. Paul Eve and several nurses.

The reputation of the road for safety was maintained in that none of the passengers received any injuries to amount to anything.
